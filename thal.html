Thalassemia

  • Thalassemia is an inherited blood disorder which manifests when the child inherits defective gene from both the parents ( who are the carriers i.e. carry single defective gene along with a normal gene but they lack the features of disease).
  • The affected person or child is not able to produce adequate hemoglobin or may form abnormal hemoglobin. Either of the situation leads to anemia. They depend on regular blood transfusions every fortnight for their survival. The patient need to undergo blood transfusion.
  • There are three types of thalassemia : Thalassemia minor donot need blood transfusion. They mostly remain asymptomatic or slightly anemic that goes un-noticed. Thalassemia intermedia may require blood transfusion. Transfusion dependent thalassemics ( TDT) may require blood transfusion within 2 years of life, later than thalassemia major. Non Transfusion dependent thalassemics ( NTDT) may not require blood transfusion or experience sudden fall of hemoglobin only during stress, fever or acute infection, pregnancy.
  • In due course of time, iron gets deposited and impair the function of affected organ. It adds on various complication like growth retardation, osteoporosis ( reuced bone density), early onset of diabetes and hypothyroidism, venous leg ulcers, delayed puberty or donot attain maturity(hypogonadism) etc. In TDT, the major source of iron is from blood transfusion. In NTDT, it is from intestinal absorption. Therefore, dietary restriction of iron is required.
  • Life long these patients need blood , iron chelators along with accessory medicines like folvite, Zinc and calcium supplements. Regular monitoring and follow up required to maintain balance between blood transfusion and Iron overload. Multidisciplinary approach is required to manage the disease complications.
    • Treatment : It is divided in three main categories :

    a) Blood Transfusion :
    According to the national guidelines of hemoglobinopathies management , thalassemia major should maintain pre-transfusion hemoglobin 9.5-10.5 gm/dl. The quality and quantity of blood transfusion matters. Fresh packed red blood cell within 5-7 days of collection according to body weight 10-15 ml/kg should be given.

    b) Iron chelators :
    Removal of iron from body is done through oral or injectable medicines. The process is known as iron chelation and the medicines used in this process are known as iron chelators. The iron gets excreted through stool and urine. The extent of iron overload may need single or dual iron chelators and at times all the three because mode of action is different. Oral chelators are Deferasirox and Defripron. Injectable iron is Desferioxamine which can be given as slow SC / IV infusion over a period of 8-12 hours a day and for 5 days/ week. These help to remove iron from circulation or from various organs. But it needs iron monitoring through blood test like serum ferritin and radiologically through T2* MRI etc. These medicines can have adverse effects, therefore, the treatment should move on recommendation of a physician.

    c) Management of complications :
    Transfusion related Alloimmunization needs extended red cell phenotyping with Rh, Kell, E, e, C, c is required on annual basis or on occasions where there is sudden increase of blood requirement, capping of pre-transfusion of hemoglobin despite of repeated transfusion. Acute management of infection, Chronic management of iron overload by T2* MRI , organ specific test for Heart, liver, kidney and endocrinology evaluation related to thyroid, gonads, pancreas should be monitored.

    d) Regular monitoring and Vaccination :
    Regular monitoring of serum ferritin, adverse events related to medicines through liver function test (LFT), renal function test ( RFT), complete cell count (CBC) , Bone marrow density through DEXA Scan , endocrine function ( especially thyroid, pancreas, gonadal) and vital organ like brain and heart. The investigations may be variable from every 3-6 months , few test at least once in a year. Annual screening for transfusion transmitted infection like HIV, Hepatitis B and C

    e) Vaccinations :
    Hepatitis B vaccination in every 5 years is compulsory for every TDT. Pre-splenectomy vaccination with Hemophilius, Pneumococcal and meningococcal is required atleast 15 days prior to the procedure of splnectomy. Post-splenectomy prophylaxis with penicillin, pneumococcal vaccine in every 5 years, influenza vaccine in every year.

    Challenges:
  • Lack of awareness about the disease
  • Beneficiaries ( number of patients) out number the resource (blood )
  • Huge gap in the implementation of prevention programme
  • High financial burden of treatment ( if not supported by Govt.)
  • Poor economic status of patients.
  • Weak diagnostic support for diagnosis and Genetic lab at Govt. level.
    • Prevention:
    Why prevention is required?
  • The increasing incidence and prevalence of thalassemia patients will result in
  • Shortage of blood resources due to life long blood dependancy
  • Complications related to Iron overload and with increasing age
  • Financial burden for the family
    • Best methods of prevention :
  • Genetic awareness
  • Pre-marital screening ( best option )
  • Pre-natal diagnosis
  • Mass screening ( on community basis)
