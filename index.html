Welcome

Where do we stand?

a. First State in India to initiate NAAT testing in All the Govt. Medical Colleges of the state and to provide free Packed-Red Cell blood to the Thalassaemia Patients. Yet, in majority of districts, the prevalence rate of HIV and HCV infection is quiet high (though routine checkups are not done) which was revealed by mass testing in camps.

b. District blood banks are well equipped with state of the art instruments and laboratory technicians, yet the Compartment separation of blood is not routinely done.

c. All Govt. hospitals have Laboratory setup, yet basic investigations essential for Thalassaemics like Ferritin, Liver Function Tests(LFT), Creatinine are not routinely checked.

d. Government of Odisha is striving to excellence in the health care sector by providing the most modern treatment, yet the myth of transfusing blood only below 8gm% is prevailing.

e. Odisha caters the maximum number of Thalassaemics yet no complete data and statistics are available.

How can we overcome?

i. Prevent the population explosion of Thalassaemics: • Organize awareness camps in the Schools, Colleges and Panchayats. • Perform Pre-marital Diagnosis programmes. • Collaborate with RMRC, Bhubaneswar or Pvt. Genetic Labs or set up its own facility for Prenatal Diagnosis in married couples who are both Thalassaemia carriers. • Every district need to have HPLC to diagnose. It will help us to target out certain non-transfusion dependent thalassemics (Intermedia& Minor), Thalassemic traits.

ii. Sufficient supply of safe blood to the Thalassaemics: • Organise blood donation camps in larger scale at all levels and also allow the private medical colleges to organize blood donation camps. • Enforce rules to provide only Packed red cells blood to the Thalassaemics at free of any cost from any private or government sectors. Compartment separation should be practiced and only packed red cells should be provided to thalassaemics. • Ensure atleast 4th Generation ELISA testing at centres where NAAT is not available.

iii. Thalassaemia Database and statistics: • To ensure proper management of any health problem, it is necessary to have data. To have an idea of how many Thalassaemics are in the state, a small move will help us to achieve the needed data. • All blood banks delivering blood units on regular basis can give the feedback that per month how many thalassaemics are regularly provided blood from their center. • This data will also help to tackle the problem related to the scarcity of Blood and Iron-Chelating drugs. • Though this will contribute only some informationbut this can be the first step to start the survey.

iv. Overall Management of Thalassaemia in the state: • To combat this dreaded disorder, Governemt should collaborate with Private and vice versa to cater hospital service to the huge population of transfusion dependent thalassemics. • Iron chelating drugs, Anti HCV and ART drugs along with Safe Blood should be made available at compensated or free rates in all health care systems (government or private). • Thalassaemiaas enlisted in the Handicapped section should now be encorporated in a Health Programme of the state or else Odisha may encounter a fatal situation in near future which will lead to huge economic burden. • And if at all it is the government has to take care then there should be standard operating procedures to obeyed strictly by physicians. • Facilities for Routine investigations should be incorporated atleast in the district hospitals so that the thalassaemics lead a disease free, productive life.

v. Collaboration with NGOs of other states: Other states those who are already advanced in their thalassemia care and management want to help us either by working with us or train our staffs. What is the procedure for it ?

A Brief note ..

Thalassemia, Hemophilia and Sickle cell desease are three social health problems, creeping silently in the society in the backdrop of social myths and fear. Thalassemia is an inherited blood disorder that causes the body to make less or abnormal hemoglobin leading to anemia requiring the patient to undergo blood transfusion every fortnight for survival while hemophilia is a sex-linked bleeding disorder where females are the carrier and males are the sufferer.

The known Thalassemia patients are the floating iceberg while the carriers remain unaware about themselves. Repeated blood transfusions lead to iron overload which, if not managed properly, result in organ damage and death.
In Odisha, there is no concrete data available on the current status on these diseases for which proper diagnosis, record keeping and maintenance of a registry is necessary. Lack of awareness, poor genetic counseling and non-availability of diagnostic facility are the main hurdles for determining the incidence and prevalence of Thalassemia.
